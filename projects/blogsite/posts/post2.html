Why I Choose Minimalism in My Design Work

When designing websites, it’s tempting to add lots of effects, animations, and visual complexity. In most of my projects, I naturally end up choosing a minimalist approach first rather than building something heavily styled.

For me, minimalism is not about making things empty or overly simple — it’s about clarity. A clean layout helps users focus on the content instead of the interface. It removes unnecessary distractions and makes the purpose of the site more immediate.

I also find that minimal design improves readability and usability. With fewer competing elements, typography, spacing, and layout become more important. This pushes me to be more intentional with structure and hierarchy instead of relying on visual effects.

There’s also a practical side to it. Minimal designs are usually easier to build, debug, and maintain. They load faster, scale better across devices, and give me more flexibility when I want to improve or expand a project later.

Over time, I’ve realised that this approach reflects how I like to design in general. I tend to start simple, focus on the core structure first, and only add complexity when it actually improves the experience rather than just filling space.
